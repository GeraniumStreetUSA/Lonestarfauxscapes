# Lone Star Flagship Guides Design

## Goal

Define a reusable flagship guide format for Lone Star Faux Scapes that feels premium, stays visually interesting, improves readability, supports sharing, and helps high-intent readers move toward a real decision.

This design is for **individual guide articles**, not for the `/guides` hub page. The hub should remain distinct and act as a curated entry point into the guide system.

## Why This Design Exists

Current Lone Star articles are informative, but many read as strong blog posts rather than premium flagship guides. The reference pattern that works better is not simply "longer content" or "more design." It uses:

- fast answers near the top
- alternating reading formats
- practical decision blocks
- visual interruption points
- clear next-step paths
- repeated utility, not repeated filler

The target outcome is a guide that readers can:

- skim without getting lost
- read deeply without fatigue
- share because sections are independently useful
- trust because claims are structured carefully

## Audience And Jobs To Be Done

Flagship guides are for readers with real decision pressure, including:

- homeowners comparing installation paths, materials, privacy solutions, or HOA approval routes
- commercial buyers comparing documentation needs, compliance questions, project scope, and install sequencing
- mixed audiences trying to understand whether a topic applies to them before they request pricing

Each flagship guide must help the reader do at least one of these:

- understand the topic quickly
- make or narrow a decision
- avoid an expensive mistake
- know what to verify before moving forward
- choose the right next step

## What Qualifies As A Flagship Guide

A page should receive flagship treatment only if it has at least one of these:

- high buyer intent
- repeated sales-call question
- repeated objection
- complex decision with real tradeoffs
- strong search demand plus strong conversion potential
- strong fit for proof, planning, or compliance-oriented guidance

Likely Lone Star flagship guide topics:

- fire ratings and documentation
- HOA approval
- DIY vs professional installation
- indoor vs outdoor living walls
- real vs artificial comparisons
- pool privacy planning
- Texas climate and material-fit topics
- cost and scope planning topics

Topics that should usually stay outside flagship treatment:

- light inspiration posts
- trend commentary
- thin local updates
- broad listicles without decision depth

## Core Design Principle

Every flagship guide should use a **fixed backbone** plus **topic-specific modules**.

The fixed backbone creates familiarity and reduces cognitive load. The custom modules keep the guide useful, distinctive, and not obviously templated.

Target ratio:

- 60-70% familiar backbone
- 30-40% topic-specific structure

## Required Backbone

Every flagship guide should include these sections:

### 1. Hero

Required elements:

- precise title
- short "who this is for" line
- one-sentence payoff
- reading time
- primary CTA
- strong visual or infographic

Purpose:

- establish relevance immediately
- signal value in the first screen
- set the tone for a premium but practical resource

### 2. Quick Answers

A compact block with 3-5 direct answers to the most immediate questions.

Purpose:

- reward skim readers immediately
- increase reading momentum
- create a screenshot-worthy utility block near the top

### 3. Decision Snapshot

A short, structured summary of what matters most.

Typical contents:

- biggest decision
- biggest risk
- what to decide first
- what usually causes mistakes

Purpose:

- orient the reader before deep reading
- reduce the feeling of "this is a long article with no map"

### 4. Core Explainer

The main educational section explaining how the topic actually works, what people misunderstand, and what context matters.

Purpose:

- build authority
- clarify nuance
- move beyond superficial SEO coverage

### 5. Next Steps

Every guide should end with 3 deliberate paths:

- pricing or consultation
- related project proof
- adjacent guide

Purpose:

- prevent dead-end reading
- move readers into the next decision

### 6. FAQ

Only high-intent, practical questions. Keep answers short and useful.

Purpose:

- capture final objections
- support search intent
- reinforce the guide as a reference asset

### Comparison Matrix

- approvals

## Reading Rhythm

Flagship guides should alternate between three modes:

### Answer Mode

Short, direct, confidence-building.

Used in:

- Quick Answers
- FAQ
- summary callouts

### Explanation Mode

Deeper prose that builds authority.

Used in:

- Core Explainer
- context sections
- nuance and tradeoff sections

### Utility Mode

Structured, visual, lower-effort sections.

Used in:

- planning grids
- comparison tables
- checklists
- timelines
- decision trees
- proof blocks

Rule:

- do not stay in one mode too long
- after 2-4 paragraphs of prose, switch format
- every major screenful should contain something scannable

## Visual System

The premium feel should come from rhythm, hierarchy, and clarity, not decorative excess.

Reusable flagship guide blocks:

- Quick Answer cards
- Decision Snapshot block
- Planning Grid
- Comparison Matrix
- Checklist / Verification box
- Proof Insert
- Next-Step rail

Each guide should contain at least two sections strong enough to stand alone as screenshots or shared visuals.

Typical candidates:

- Quick Answers
- Decision Snapshot
- Planning Grid
- Comparison Matrix
- Documentation Checklist

## Truthfulness Rules

This design must align with Lone Star's stronger truthfulness standard.

Every flagship guide should contain:

### 1. One Real Decision

The page must help the reader decide something concrete.

### 2. One Real Tension

Examples:

- budget vs finish
- speed vs customization
- aesthetics vs compliance
- privacy vs openness
- DIY savings vs install risk

### 3. One Proof Anchor

Examples:

- published project profile
- product or documentation reality
- verified planning constraint
- real site-condition dependency

### 4. One Truthfulness Checkpoint

Explicitly state what is conditional.

Typical Lone Star conditions:

- site conditions
- product line
- HOA approval
- AHJ review
- substrate and mounting conditions
- direct sun and exposure
- access and dimensions

### 5. One Guided Next Step

The page must clearly show what to do next.

## Internal Linking Rules

Each flagship guide should link to:

- one relevant service page
- one relevant project proof page
- one adjacent guide
- one conversion page, usually pricing or contact

This prevents flagship guides from feeling isolated and makes them work as part of a larger system.

## Shareability Rules

Every flagship guide should include:

- one section people would screenshot because it is useful
- one section people would share because it resolves confusion
- one section that makes the brand look credible through clarity, not hype

If a section cannot stand alone visually or conceptually, it is probably too text-heavy.

## Tone Rules

The tone should feel:

- practical first
- premium second
- persuasive through clarity
- specific without sounding rigid
- confident without overclaiming

Avoid:

- oversized hype sections
- decorative but empty components
- repeated sales framing
- unsupported certainty

## Hub Page Note

This design does **not** mean the `/guides` hub should look like an article.

The hub should stay unique and act as:

- a curated entry point
- a guide selector
- a topical map
- a path into flagship resources

The premium flagship treatment belongs on the guide articles themselves.

## Initial Rollout Recommendation

Start by applying this structure to a small first set of flagship guides instead of trying to convert every guide at once.

Recommended first-wave candidates:

- NFPA 701 vs ASTM E84
- DIY vs professional artificial hedge installation
- How to get HOA approval
- Indoor vs outdoor artificial living walls

This will give Lone Star a representative set across:

- compliance
- installation
- approval friction
- product comparison

## Success Criteria

The design succeeds if flagship guides:

- feel easier to skim than current linear guides
- contain more visually distinct utility sections
- create clearer handoffs to pricing, proof, and next guides
- support sharing through standalone visual blocks
- preserve truthfulness while improving perceived quality

## Non-Goals

This design does not aim to:

- make every article a flagship guide
- make the `/guides` hub look like an article page
- replace all article variation with a rigid template
- rely on visual polish without real informational value
